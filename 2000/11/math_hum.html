The following is a proof that the perpendicular bisectors of the three edges of \(\triangle \mathrm{ABC}\) meet at a single point.
<Proof>
Consider line \(\mathrm{BC}\) as the \(x\)-axis and the perpendicular bisector of edge \(\mathrm{BC}\) as the \(y\)-axis, and let \(\mathrm{A}(a, b)\), \(\mathrm{B}(-c, 0)\) and \(\mathrm{C}(c, 0).\) (※ \(b \ne 0, c > 0\))
(i) If \(a \ne c\) and \(a \ne -c\)
The slope of line \(\mathrm{AC}\) is \(\dfrac{b}{a-c}\), so the equation of the line passing through the midpoint \(\mathrm{E}\) of edge \(\mathrm{AC}\) perpendicular to edge \(\mathrm{AC}\), is
\(y=\fbox{\(\;(\alpha)\;\)}\left(\!\!x-\dfrac{a+c}{2}\!\!\right)\!+\!\dfrac{b}{2} =\fbox{\(\;(\alpha)\;\)}x + \fbox{\(\;(\beta)\;\)}\)
①
Similarly, the equation of the line passing through the midpoint \(\mathrm{D}\) of edge \(\mathrm{AB}\) perpendicular to edge \(\mathrm{AB}\), is
\(y= -\dfrac{a+c}{b}x + \fbox{\(\;(\beta)\;\)}\)
②
The two lines ① and ② have the same \(y\)-intercept, so the three perpendicular bisectors meet at point \((0, \fbox{\(\;(\beta)\;\)})\) on the \(y\)-axis.
Therefore, the perpendicular bisectors of the three edges of \(\triangle \mathrm{ABC}\) meet at a single point.
(ii) If \(a = c\) or \(a = -c\)
\(\triangle \mathrm{ABC}\) is \(\fbox{\(\;(\gamma)\;\)}\), and the three perpendicular bisectors meet at either \(\mathrm{D}\) or \(\mathrm{E}\).
Therefore, the perpendicular bisectors of the three edges of \(\triangle \mathrm{ABC}\) meet at a single point.

  2. \(-\dfrac{a-c}{b}\),  \(\dfrac{a^2+b^2-c^2}{2b}\),  an equilateral triangle
  3. \(-\dfrac{a-c}{b}\),  \(\dfrac{-a^2+b^2-c^2}{2b}\),  an isosceles triangle
  4. \(\dfrac{a-c}{b}\),  \(\dfrac{a^2+b^2-c^2}{2b}\),  an isosceles triangle

The following is a proof that there are infinitely many primes of the form \(4k+3\).
(※ \(k\) is a nonnegative integer.)
<Proof>
Suppose there are finitely many primes of the form \(4k+3\), and let them be \(3, 7, 11, 19, \cdots, p\).
Suppose \(n=4(7 \cdot 11 \cdot 19 \cdot \;\cdots\; \cdot p)+3\). Then \(n\) is \(\fbox{\(\;(\alpha)\;\)}\) by numbers \(3, 7, 11, 19, \cdots, p\). All prime factors of \(n\) are integers of the form \(4k+1\) or \(4k+3\), and the product of two integers of the form \(4k+1\) is an integer of the form \(\fbox{\(\;(\beta)\;\)}\). Therefore, if all prime factors of \(n\) are of the form \(\fbox{\(\;(\beta)\;\)}\), then \(n\) is also of the form \(\fbox{\(\;(\beta)\;\)}\). This is a contradiction, thus \(n\) has a prime factor \(q\) of the form \(\fbox{\(\;(\gamma)\;\)}\). \(n\) is divisible by \(q\), so \(q\) is a prime of the form \(4k+3\) which is different from all of \(3, 7, 11, 19, \cdots, p\). This contradicts the premise.
Therefore, there are infinitely many primes of the form \(4k+3\).
In the proof above, what are appropriate for \((\alpha), (\beta)\) and \((\gamma)\) in this order? [2 points]
  1. divisible,  \(4k+1\),  \(4k+1\)
  2. divisible,  \(4k+3\),  \(4k+3\)
  3. not divisible,  \(4k+3\),  \(4k+1\)
  4. not divisible,  \(4k+1\),  \(4k+1\)
  5. not divisible,  \(4k+1\),  \(4k+3\)
